When I brought my first puppy home, I had a color-coded spreadsheet on my fridge. I expected a linear progression: Week 8, he masters his name; Week 10, he sits on command; Week 12, he’s potty trained. By the third week, my spreadsheet was a mess of erasable ink and frustration. My puppy wasn’t broken, but my rigid timeline was.

If you are currently feeling like your dog is “behind” because they aren’t hitting the milestones you saw in a YouTube video or a training book, take a breath. Dogs don’t read training manuals. They live in the moment, and their learning curve is almost never a straight line. Here is how to adjust your expectations and your schedule when progress stalls.

Identify the “Invisible” Friction

Before you assume your dog is stubborn or slow, look for the environmental factors that disrupt their learning. Often, we blame the puppy’s intelligence when the issue is actually our own timing or environment.

For example, I spent a week trying to teach my Lab mix “stay” in the middle of our living room. It was a disaster. I realized later that the living room is where the kids play, the cat runs by, and the front door slams every few minutes. He wasn’t failing the command; he was failing the distraction test because I hadn’t set the bar low enough.

If a milestone is missed, drop your requirements by 50%. If you are trying to teach “down” and your puppy won’t listen, go back to “sit.” If they can’t hold a stay for five seconds, try one second. If they can’t do it in the park, move to the quiet hallway. Success builds confidence, and confidence is what eventually leads to mastery.

The “Potty Regression” Reality Check

Potty training is the milestone that causes the most anxiety. You’ll have a week of perfect performance followed by a day where your puppy treats your rug like a park.

This isn’t always a behavioral regression. Often, it is a physical or environmental change. Did you change their food? Did the weather turn cold and rainy, making them reluctant to step outside? Is there a new sound—like a neighbor mowing their lawn—that makes them nervous about stepping onto the grass?

When regression happens, reset the clock. Treat them like they are eight weeks old again. Increase the frequency of trips outside, reward them heavily with high-value treats (like small pieces of boiled chicken) the second they finish, and go back to tethering them to you inside the house. If they are in your sight, they aren’t having accidents. Two weeks of “reset” is usually enough to get them back on track.

Adjusting Training Sessions for Brain Fatigue

If your puppy seems to be getting worse at commands they previously knew, they might be mentally exhausted. Training is physically draining for a young dog. A 15-minute session for a four-month-old puppy is a heavy cognitive load.

Watch for these specific signs that it’s time to stop the session:

  • The “Zoomies”: If your puppy starts bouncing off the walls or nipping at your clothes, their brain has effectively shut off. They aren’t being naughty; they are overstimulated.
  • The “Check-out”: If they stop looking at the treats and start sniffing the floor or staring at a wall, they are done.
  • Repetitive Mistakes: If they offer a “paw” when you ask for “sit” three times in a row, they are guessing because they are tired.

When you see these signs, end the session on a “win.” Ask for one very simple command they know perfectly, like “touch” or “sit,” reward them, and put them in their crate or playpen for a nap. Training while a dog is frustrated is a waste of time and actually creates negative associations with the work.

How to Pivot Your Weekly Routine

If you find that your current schedule isn’t yielding results, stop forcing it. If your evenings are chaotic, don’t try to cram training into a 6:00 PM slot.

Try these micro-adjustments instead:

  • The 3-Minute Rule: Instead of one 20-minute session, do four 3-minute sessions throughout the day. It’s easier to keep a puppy’s focus for 180 seconds than it is for 20 minutes.
  • Life-Reward Training: Stop giving your puppy a full bowl of food. Use their mealtime kibble as rewards for good behavior throughout the day. If they want to go outside, ask for a sit. If they want to play, ask for a down. This makes training a part of their existence, not just a “chore” you do at a specific time.
  • The Saturday Reset: Use your weekend to focus on one specific struggle. Spend five minutes just observing your dog’s triggers without asking for a single command. Understanding why they react to a specific sound or person is more valuable than teaching them a new trick.

Patience is a technical skill, not just a personality trait. When you stop looking at the calendar and start looking at the dog in front of you, you will realize that “missing a milestone” is just an invitation to try a different approach.

Your next steps should involve evaluating where your training sessions are currently failing. Are you asking for too much, too soon, or in too distracting an environment? Take a few days to simplify your routine and focus on re-establishing the bond between you and your puppy before moving on to more complex commands.
