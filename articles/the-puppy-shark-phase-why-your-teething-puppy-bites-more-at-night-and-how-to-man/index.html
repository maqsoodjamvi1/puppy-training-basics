The 'Puppy Shark' Phase: Why Your Teething Puppy Bites More at Night and How to Manage It

If you’ve recently brought home a puppy between the ages of 12 and 20 weeks, you’ve likely experienced the “witching hour.” This is when your adorable, sleepy fluffball suddenly transforms into a land-shark, darting around the living room, lunging at your ankles, and turning your pant legs into shredded ribbons.

This behavior is rarely about aggression. It is almost always a combination of biology, teething discomfort, and a frantic attempt to burn off pent-up energy before sleep. Here is why it happens and how you can actually survive it.

Why the Nighttime Frenzy Happens

Puppies don’t have an “off” switch. In the wild, they would sleep in short bursts throughout the day and night. When you bring them into a human household, their internal clock is often out of sync with your schedule.

By the time 7:00 PM or 8:00 PM rolls around, your puppy is likely overtired. Much like a toddler who skips a nap, an overtired puppy loses the ability to self-regulate. Their brain chemistry shifts, and they experience a surge of cortisol and adrenaline. Add in the physical pain of incoming adult teeth—which are sharp, pressurized, and constantly aching—and you have the perfect recipe for a biting spree. They aren’t trying to be “naughty”; they are physically overstimulated and looking for relief.

The Strategy: The “Redirection-to-Rest” Protocol

When the shark mode kicks in, stop talking. If you yell “no” or “ow,” you are often just adding more noise and excitement to a situation that is already over-arousing for the puppy. Instead, follow this three-step process:

  1. The Silent Redirect: Keep a high-value, frozen chew toy within arm’s reach at all times during the evening. When the puppy lunges for your hand, don’t pull back quickly—that mimics prey movement and makes the game more fun. Instead, calmly place the frozen toy directly into their mouth.
  2. The “Boredom” Exit: If the redirection fails and they keep targeting your skin, remove yourself from the equation. Step behind a baby gate or into a bathroom for 30 seconds. By removing the “toy” (you), you teach the puppy that biting causes the play to end instantly.
  3. The Forced Decompression: If the behavior continues despite your best efforts, your puppy is telling you they are exhausted. It is time for a crate or a playpen nap. This isn’t a punishment; it’s a reset. Place them in their quiet, darkened area with a safe chew. Most puppies will be asleep within five minutes once the environment is calm.

Managing the Teething Pain

The physical act of chewing releases endorphins, which act as a natural painkiller for sore gums. If your puppy is chewing you, they are essentially self-medicating. To stay one step ahead of the shark phase, be proactive about what you offer them:

  • Frozen Carrots: Take a whole, raw carrot and freeze it. The cold numbs the gums, and the crunch provides the exact pressure they crave.
  • Wet Towel Knots: Take a clean kitchen towel, soak it in water, tie it into a loose knot, and freeze it. The texture of the fabric against their teeth feels incredibly satisfying compared to the smooth surface of a rubber toy.
  • Frozen Broth Cubes: Make low-sodium chicken or beef broth cubes in an ice tray. Drop one on the floor during the witching hour. It takes them a while to lick and chew through the ice, which keeps them occupied and focused away from your ankles.

Adjusting Your Evening Routine

If you find the biting starts at the same time every night, you are likely missing the “pre-game” window. Instead of waiting for the frenzy to start, initiate a calm-down routine 30 minutes earlier.

Lower the lights in the room. Turn off the television or switch to low-volume, calm music. If you have been doing high-intensity play—like tug-of-war or chasing—stop doing those activities by 6:00 PM. Replace them with “sniffing” games. Hide small treats around a rug or use a snuffle mat. Sniffing is mentally exhausting for a puppy and forces them to slow their heart rate, which is the exact opposite of the state they reach during a biting frenzy.

Consistency is Your Greatest Asset

It is tempting to try different techniques every night, but puppies thrive on predictability. If you decide that biting means the “fun” ends, stick to that rule every single time. If you allow them to bite your slippers on Tuesday but punish them for it on Wednesday, you will only create confusion.

Remember that this phase is temporary. As their adult teeth fully emerge—usually by the five- or six-month mark—the obsessive need to chew will naturally subside. You are currently in the training trenches, but by focusing on redirecting their energy toward appropriate surfaces and enforcing quiet time when they lose control, you will make it through the shark phase with your skin intact.

As you look toward the next few weeks of development, consider how you can further refine your training environment to support these calmer, more settled behaviors. Evaluating your current setup is the best way to ensure you are ready for the next milestone.
