Puppy Biting During Play: How to Teach Your Dog to Soften Their Mouth

If you have brought a puppy home in the last few weeks, you are likely sporting a collection of tiny, crescent-shaped scratches on your hands and ankles. It is the classic “land shark” phase. While it feels aggressive when your puppy lunges at your shoelaces or latches onto your wrist, I want to reassure you: this is normal developmental behavior. Puppies explore the world with their mouths because they lack hands.

The goal isn’t to stop your puppy from putting their mouth on things entirely—that’s how they interact with toys—but to teach them “bite inhibition.” You want your dog to learn that human skin is fragile and that teeth should never apply pressure during play.

Why “Ouch!” Doesn’t Always Work

You might have read advice suggesting you should yelp like a littermate whenever your puppy bites you. In my experience, this is hit-or-miss. For some puppies, a high-pitched “ouch” sounds exactly like a squeaky toy, which actually triggers their prey drive and makes them bite harder or faster.

Instead of relying on vocalizations that might excite the dog, rely on the withdrawal of attention. Your puppy bites because they want to engage with you. If the biting makes the engagement stop, the puppy learns that teeth are the ultimate “off switch” for fun.

The “Three-Strike” Rule for Playtime

I use a simple system when training a puppy to soften their mouth. When we are playing on the floor, I keep a close watch on their intensity.

  1. Strike One: The puppy puts their mouth on me. I immediately go limp. I stop moving my hands and become as boring as a statue. Often, the puppy will pause because the “toy” (me) stopped moving. If they let go, I wait three seconds, then re-engage with a toy.
  2. Strike Two: If they latch on again, I give a calm, neutral “too bad,” and I stand up, crossing my arms. I turn my back to the puppy for about 10 to 15 seconds. I don’t scold them; I just remove my presence.
  3. Strike Three: If they follow me and continue to bite, I calmly step over a baby gate or move into another room, closing the door behind me for 30 seconds.

The key here is consistency. If you let them bite you hard four times but ignore them on the fifth, you have just taught them that they have to bite harder to get your attention.

Redirection: The “Swap” Technique

It is unrealistic to expect a puppy to never put their mouth on you if you don’t give them a better alternative. I always keep a “decoy” toy in my pocket or within arm’s reach during training sessions.

When the puppy goes for my fingers, I don’t pull away quickly, which triggers a chase instinct. Instead, I gently place a long tug toy or a stuffed animal directly against their mouth. As soon as they take the toy, I start playing with them using that object.

The goal is to teach them: “My hands are for petting and feeding, but this toy is for biting.” If they cannot make the switch to the toy, the game ends. You are essentially teaching them that the toy is the only “legal” thing to bite during a session.

Managing the “Witching Hour”

Most puppy biting happens in the evening, often called the “witching hour.” This is usually when your puppy is overtired. A puppy that hasn’t slept enough is like a toddler who missed their nap; they lose their impulse control.

If your puppy is zooming around the room, nipping at your heels, and generally refusing to listen to commands they usually know, they don’t need more training—they need a nap. Place them in their crate or a quiet playpen with a chew toy. Once they settle down, you will find that their mouthy behavior decreases significantly.

What to Avoid During Training

There are a few common mistakes that will stall your progress:

  • Pushing them away: When you push a puppy off you, it feels like roughhousing to them. It encourages them to push back.
  • Wiggling your fingers: Don’t let your fingers move like prey. Keep them still or tuck them into your armpits.
  • Physical correction: Never hit or pin your puppy. This creates fear. A fearful puppy is much more likely to use their teeth defensively later in life.

Teaching a puppy to have a “soft mouth” is a slow process that requires a lot of patience. It won’t happen overnight, and you will likely have days where you feel like you aren’t making any progress. Keep your sessions short—five minutes is plenty—and end on a positive note whenever possible.

As you navigate this stage, you will notice that your puppy gradually becomes more aware of your boundaries. Once you have the basics of bite inhibition under control, you can begin focusing on more complex impulse control exercises, such as teaching your dog to wait for a toy or settle on a mat.
