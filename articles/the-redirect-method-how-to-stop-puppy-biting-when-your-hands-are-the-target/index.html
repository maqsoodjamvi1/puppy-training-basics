If you have a puppy, you are likely sporting a collection of tiny, red scratches on your forearms and ankles. It’s a rite of passage for new owners, but that doesn’t make it any less frustrating. When your puppy treats your skin like a chew toy, the most effective tool in your kit isn’t a firm “no”—it’s the redirect.

The redirect method works because it respects biology. Puppies explore the world through their mouths, and when they are overstimulated or teething, they have a physical need to gnash on something. The goal isn’t to stop the behavior entirely, but to teach the puppy that your skin is off-limits and that specific toys are the only acceptable targets for their teeth.

The First Rule: Never Use Your Hands as Bait

The biggest mistake I see new owners make is using their hands to wrestle with a puppy. It’s tempting when they are small and cute, but you are accidentally teaching them that human skin is an appropriate “prey item.”

If you use your fingers to wiggle in front of their face to get them to play, you are confirming that your hands are part of the game. From this day forward, stop using your hands to initiate play. If your puppy starts to nip, your hands should become “dead.” Pull them into your armpits or stand up and cross your arms. Do not pull away quickly or shriek, as high-pitched noises and sudden movement often trigger a puppy’s prey drive, making them think you are playing a game of “catch me if you can.”

How to Execute a Perfect Redirect

The redirect method requires you to be two steps ahead of your puppy at all times. Keep a toy within reach—on the sofa next to you, in your pocket, or tucked into your waistband—whenever you are in the same room as your puppy.

When the puppy lunges for your hand, follow this exact sequence:

  • Interrupt: Say a calm, low-toned “Oops” or “Too bad.”
  • Offer: Immediately present a toy. The toy should be slightly more interesting than your hand. If it’s a rope, give it a little wiggle. If it’s a stuffed toy, make it “run” along the floor away from the puppy.
  • Praise: The moment the puppy’s teeth touch the toy instead of your skin, offer calm, verbal praise. Let them engage with the toy for a few seconds.

If they refuse the toy and go back for your skin, it is time for a “reset.” Calmly stand up and step over a baby gate or move into another room for 30 seconds. You aren’t punishing the puppy; you are simply removing the toy (you) from the situation. They will quickly learn that biting your hand causes the fun to stop immediately.

Why Texture and Temperature Matter

Sometimes a redirect fails because the toy you’ve chosen is boring. If you are sitting on the couch and the puppy is biting your hand, offering a hard, smooth plastic bone might not cut it. They want something that offers resistance.

If your puppy is in a “shark” mood, look for toys that provide sensory feedback:

  • Braided ropes: These provide the tension that a puppy’s jaw craves.
  • Soft, plush toys: These are great for “kill shaking,” which satisfies their natural instinct to tear at prey.
  • Frozen rubber toys: If your puppy is teething, their gums are likely inflamed. A frozen rubber toy provides a cooling sensation that actually soothes their pain, making it a much better target than your soft hand.

Keep a “rotation” of three or four toys in different rooms. If you find yourself in the kitchen and the puppy goes for your ankles, you should be able to grab a toy from the floor within two seconds. If you have to walk across the house to find one, the puppy has already succeeded in biting you, and the window for a successful redirect has closed.

Knowing When to Fold

There is a major difference between a puppy who is bored and a puppy who is overtired. If you have tried redirecting three or four times in a row and the puppy is still lunging at you, whining, or acting like a Tasmanian devil, they are almost certainly overstimulated.

Puppies don’t always know how to settle down on their own. If they reach this level of manic energy, they don’t need more play; they need a nap. Place them in their crate or an exercise pen with a chew, turn the lights down, and let them decompress. You will often find that the biting problem disappears entirely once they get the sleep they actually needed.

Consistency is the hardest part of this process. It is exhausting to have to monitor your puppy’s mouth every second of the day, but by providing a clear, immediate alternative every time they make a mistake, you are building a foundation of good manners. You are teaching them that your hands are for petting and treats, while their toys are for biting.

As you implement these changes, you will likely notice that your puppy starts looking for their toys before they even come toward you. Understanding the “why” behind the bite is the first step toward a peaceful home, but the real progress happens when you move from reactive correction to proactive play.
