The 'Name Game' Method: How to Teach Your Dog Focus Before Starting Basic Commands

Most people start training by trying to force a “sit” or a “stay” the moment they bring a new dog home. I’ve been there, and I can tell you that it’s a recipe for frustration. You’re asking for complex movements before you’ve established the most important communication channel: the dog’s attention.

Before your dog will ever reliably “sit” or “heel,” they need to know that listening to you is the most rewarding thing they can do. I call this the “Name Game.” It isn’t a trick; it’s a foundational focus exercise that turns your dog’s name into a signal that means, “Something great is about to happen, look at me.”

Why the Name Game is Step Zero

When you call your dog’s name in a chaotic environment, you’re competing with squirrels, other dogs, and interesting smells. If your dog’s name has become “background noise”—something you say repeatedly while they ignore you—the name has lost its power.

The Name Game resets this. It teaches your dog that their name is a trigger word. When they hear it, they should immediately snap their head toward your eyes. You aren’t just teaching them to respond to a sound; you are teaching them to seek your guidance. Once you have their eyes, you have their brain. Without eye contact, you’re just shouting at a dog who is busy sniffing a leaf.

How to Play: The Kitchen Method

Start this in a boring room with zero distractions. Your living room or kitchen is perfect. Do not try this at the dog park on day one.

  1. Load the clicker or marker. If you use a clicker, click it and immediately give a treat. If you don’t, use a verbal marker like “Yes!” followed immediately by a treat. Do this 10 times until the dog understands that the marker predicts food.
  2. The “Look” moment. Wait for your dog to be doing something else—sniffing the floor, looking out the window, or just standing there. Say their name exactly once. Don’t repeat it.
  3. The reward. As soon as your dog’s head whips around toward you, mark it (“Yes!”) and treat.
  4. Repeat, but keep it short. Do this about 10 to 15 times in a session, then stop. You want them to be hungry for more, not bored.

The goal is to get a fast, enthusiastic “head-snap” toward you every time they hear their name. If your dog doesn’t look, you’re likely in an environment that is too distracting. Move to a quieter room and try again.

Adding the “Distraction Factor”

Once your dog is reliably snapping their head toward you in the kitchen, you have to raise the bar. Real life isn’t a quiet kitchen.

If you are outside in the backyard, don’t just say their name while they are staring at a fence post. Wait for that split second when they are slightly less focused on the fence—maybe they pause to take a breath—and then say their name. When they turn, give them a high-value reward, like a small piece of plain boiled chicken or a tiny hunk of cheese, rather than their standard kibble.

If you call their name and they don’t look, do not repeat the name five times. You are teaching them that their name is optional. Instead, walk backward a few steps, make a kissing noise, or clap your hands lightly to grab their attention. Once they look, mark it and treat. You’re rewarding the focus, not the obedience to the name itself.

Troubleshooting Common Mistakes

The most common mistake I see is owners using their dog’s name as a “nag.” If you say “Bella, Bella, Bella, Bella,” you are training the dog to tune you out. Your dog’s name should be a positive, high-value cue.

Another mistake is calling the dog’s name to do something they hate, like getting a bath or getting their nails trimmed. If you only call them for things they dislike, they will eventually stop looking at you when they hear their name. For the first few weeks, use their name only for positive interactions—food, play, or gentle praise.

Keep your sessions under five minutes. Dogs, especially puppies, lose interest quickly. It is much better to do three 2-minute sessions throughout the day than one long, grueling 15-minute session that ends with both of you feeling annoyed.

Moving Toward Real-World Application

Once you have mastered the “head-snap” in your backyard, you can start using it on walks. If your dog gets fixated on a person or another dog across the street, use the Name Game. Say their name, and the moment they turn toward you, mark and move in the opposite direction. You are essentially turning the name into a “reset” button that breaks their fixation and brings their focus back to the handler.

By mastering this, you aren’t just teaching a command; you are building a habit of checking in with you. This creates a “default” behavior where your dog looks at you whenever they are unsure or overstimulated.

Now that you have a solid way to capture your dog’s focus on command, you’re ready to begin layering in the more traditional obedience cues that rely on that foundation of attention.
