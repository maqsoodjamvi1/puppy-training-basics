When I brought home my second rescue dog, a high-energy mixed breed who didn’t know his own name, I made the classic rookie mistake. I tried to teach him everything at once. On day one, I was trying to lure him into a “stay” while simultaneously dangling a piece of cheese for “down” and shouting “come” from across the kitchen. The result was a very confused puppy, a frustrated human, and a lot of wasted string cheese.

Dogs do not learn language the way we do. They learn through patterns, consequences, and context. If you throw a jumble of different expectations at a dog before they understand how to learn, you will both end up burnt out. Teaching basic obedience is a lot like building a house; if you try to put the roof on before the foundation is poured, the whole thing collapses.

Here is the exact sequence I now use with every dog I foster and train, and why teaching these commands in a specific order unlocks faster learning.

Step 1: Name Recognition and Attention (The Zero Command)

Before you teach a dog to sit, lie down, or stay, you have to teach them that paying attention to you pays off. If your dog is looking at a squirrel, a crumb on the floor, or the television, any command you give is just white noise.

Start indoors in a quiet room with zero distractions. Say your dog’s name in a normal, upbeat tone. The exact microsecond their eyes lock onto yours, mark the behavior with a verbal “Yes!” or a clicker, and hand them a pea-sized piece of high-value food like plain chicken or roast beef.

Do this ten times a session, a few times a day. You are conditioning a neurological reflex: “My name means look at the human, and looking at the human means good things happen.” Once your dog snaps their head toward you reliably every single time you say their name, even if there is a mild distraction nearby, you are ready to move on.

Step 2: Sit (The Impulse Control Anchor)

I start with “sit” because it is the ultimate currency exchange in a dog’s world. A sit is physically incompatible with a lot of bad behaviors, like jumping up on guests or bolting through an open door. It forces a dog to pause and engage their brain instead of reacting on pure impulse.

To teach this without pushing on their rear end—which often causes them to push back—use a food lure. Hold a treat right at your dog’s nose, then slowly move it backward over their head toward their ears. As their nose follows the treat up, their rear end naturally lowers to the floor. The moment their hindquarters touch the ground, say “Yes!”, hand over the treat, and let them eat.

Add the verbal cue “Sit” right as you start moving the lure. Within a couple of short sessions, you can phase out the massive hand motion and turn it into a subtle hand signal.

Step 3: Come (The Lifesaving Recall)

A reliable recall is the most important safety skill your dog will ever learn. Because of this, it needs to be taught early, while your dog is still small and heavily motivated to stick close to you.

The golden rule of teaching “Come” is that it should never, ever be associated with anything unpleasant. If you call your dog to you and then immediately clip their nails, put them in a bath, or lock them in their crate, you just poisoned the cue.

  • Practice in a safe, enclosed area like a hallway or a fenced yard.
  • Crouch down low, open your arms wide, and say “Come” in a joyful, high-pitched voice.
  • Make yourself look like the most exciting thing in the universe by patting your legs or running backward a few steps.
  • When they barrel into you, throw a mini-party. Feed them three separate small treats in a row while praising them enthusiastically.

Step 4: Down (The Relaxation Trigger)

Once your dog understands “Sit” and “Come,” you can introduce “Down,” which asks for a much higher level of vulnerability. To a dog, lying flat on their belly exposes their vulnerable underside. If they are nervous, high-strung, or distrustful, they will resist this position.

Start with your dog in a reliable sit. Hold a treat right at their nose, then slowly lower your hand straight down between their front paws. Once your hand hits the floor, slide it slowly forward along the ground. Their nose will follow the treat down, and their front legs will slide forward into a sphinx position.

If your dog pops back up immediately, that is okay. Keep the treat between their paws for a second or two, saying “Good stay, good down,” before releasing them with an “Okay!” cue.

Step 5: Stay (The Duration Builder)

Trying to teach “Stay” on day one is a recipe for failure because it requires three separate elements at once: duration, distance, and distraction. By putting “Stay” at the end of this sequence, your dog already understands how to sit and lie down on command. Now, we are simply asking them to hold that position for a few seconds longer.

Ask your dog to sit or down. Raise one hand flat like a stop sign, say “Stay,” and take one step back. If they hold the position even for a single second, step right back to them, say “Yes!”, and reward them.

Never call your dog out of a stay; always walk back to them to deliver the reward. If you call them, you are accidentally teaching them that “Stay” actually means “Stay until I say come.” Gradually increase the number of steps you take backward, working your way up to a minute or more before adding distractions like walking around them or bouncing a ball nearby.

Getting the sequence right transforms dog training from a frustrating battle of wills into a clear, predictable language game that your dog can easily win.
