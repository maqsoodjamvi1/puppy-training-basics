Apartment Puppy Training Schedule: Housebreaking and Crate Training by the Week

Bringing a puppy home to an apartment is a unique logistical puzzle. Without a fenced-in backyard waiting just past the door, every single potty break requires shoes, keys, an elevator ride, and a brisk walk to a patch of grass. When I brought my golden retriever mix, Otis, home to a third-floor city apartment, I quickly realized that a loose, “go-with-the-flow” approach was a recipe for stained rugs and endless frustration.

Success in tight quarters comes down to a strict, predictable routine. By breaking the first four home-coming weeks into a concrete schedule, you can build bladder control, prevent destructive indoor habits, and help your puppy learn the rhythm of apartment living.

Week 1: The 45-Minute Clock and Crate Comfort

Your primary goal during the first week is survival and establishing a baseline. A young puppy’s bladder is tiny, and the shock of a new environment will disrupt their natural rhythms.

Start by setting a timer on your phone for every 45 minutes during the day. When it rings, scoop your puppy up—do not let them walk, as they often start leaking the second they get excited—and carry them straight to your designated outdoor relief spot.

When you return inside, the puppy goes directly into their crate if you cannot give them 100% of your attention. For week one, the crate should be just large enough for them to stand up, turn around, and lie down. If it is too big, they will use one corner as a bathroom and sleep in the other.

  • Feed all meals inside the crate to build positive associations with the space.
  • Keep the crate in your bedroom at night so you can hear whimpering and take them out for a fast, boring 2:00 AM bathroom trip.
  • Do not coddle or play during night trips; it is strictly business.

Week 2: Expanding Windows and Tethering Indoors

By week two, you should start noticing small patterns. You might realize your puppy can comfortably hold it for 60 minutes instead of 45, especially if they are sleeping.

However, week two is usually when puppies try to sneak away to chew shoes or pee behind the couch while you are washing dishes. To stop this without constantly yelling “no,” use the indoor tether method. Clip a lightweight six-foot leash to your puppy’s flat collar and loop the handle around your belt loop or wrist as you move around the apartment.

  • The benefit: Your puppy is physically prevented from wandering into another room to make a mistake.
  • The lesson: They learn that where you go, they go, which lays the foundation for future loose-leash walking in tight hallways and lobbies.

Continue your outdoor trips immediately after waking up, right after intense play sessions, and 15 minutes after eating or drinking. When they eliminate outside, throw a high-value treat party—real chicken or small bits of cheese—the exact second they finish, before you even head back inside.

Week 3: Dealing with Hallway Accidents and Leash Cues

Apartment living introduces a specific hurdle: the common hallway, elevator, and lobby. Many puppies struggle to hold their bladders while waiting for a slow elevator or walking down a carpeted corridor, leading to frustrating lobby accidents just feet from freedom.

In week three, you need to tighten your pre-trip routine.

Stop letting your puppy get excited before you leave the apartment. If you throw a toy or get them riled up right before grabbing the leash, their heart rate spikes and their bladder muscles relax. Instead, keep your energy completely neutral. Put the leash on while they are sitting calmly. Carry them down the hallway if they are small enough, or walk briskly with a handful of high-value treats held right at your side to keep their focus entirely on you rather than the hallway smells.

If you hit a plateau where accidents happen inside again, reset your clock. Drop back down to 45-minute intervals for a few days to rebuild their confidence and physical capacity.

Week 4: Independence and Trust Building

By week four, your puppy should understand the basic mechanics of housebreaking: outside is where relief happens, and the crate is a safe, clean den. Now, you can begin testing small amounts of unsupervised freedom in puppy-proofed zones.

Instead of strict tethering, block off a small area—like the kitchen—using baby gates. Leave your puppy alone in that space for just five minutes while you stand quietly on the other side of the door. Gradually build this time up to 15, 30, and 45 minutes.

If they stay quiet and keep the floor dry, reward them calmly when you return. If they bark or have an accident, you moved too fast; shrink the space or reduce the time duration for a few more days.

Consistency during these first four weeks sets the permanent tone for your dog’s life in a shared building. As you evaluate your daily routine and look ahead to advanced obedience, leash manners, and socialization around neighbors and other dogs, having the right gear and step-by-step guidance makes all the difference.
